New Developed Country Criteria

Spring Park, Joonseop Jang, Oscar Molina Romualdo
George Mason University Korea

Abstract

Countries are often labeled as “developed” using income-based thresholds such as GNI per capita. However, income alone may not fully capture the broader social conditions associated with development. In this project, we examine whether social indicators such as fertility, infant mortality, migrant share, aging, and service-sector employment align with conventional GNI-based classifications.

Using World Bank World Development Indicators and country groupings from the UN World Economic Situation and Prospects framework, we compare patterns across countries to show that development is multidimensional and that broader social indicators may reveal important differences that income-based definitions overlook.

1. Fertility and Development

Fertility decline is one of the most widely observed demographic patterns associated with development. Lower fertility often emerges alongside urbanization, women’s education, rising living costs, and expanded access to healthcare and family planning.

Developed economies generally cluster at lower fertility levels, while many developing economies remain higher. However, the charts also reveal important variation: some middle-income countries have already undergone major fertility declines, while some wealthy economies do not fit a simple demographic pattern. This suggests that demographic transition is strongly related to development, but not perfectly captured by income category alone.

2. Global Infant Mortality

Infant mortality is one of the clearest indicators of social development because it reflects healthcare access, maternal health, nutrition, sanitation, and public infrastructure. Unlike income alone, it captures whether people experience the benefits of development in everyday life.

The map shows a strong geographic divide. Developed countries generally have very low infant mortality rates, while much higher rates remain concentrated in parts of Sub-Saharan Africa and other lower-income regions. This provides strong evidence that health outcomes are deeply tied to development and should play a role in how development is defined.

3. Migrant Share by Development Group

This box plot compares international migrant stock as a share of total population across developed economies, economies in transition, and developing economies. Migration can reflect labor-market attraction, economic opportunity, political stability, and global integration, all of which are often associated with development.

Developed economies generally show the highest migrant shares and the greatest variation. Economies in transition tend to fall in the middle, while most developing economies remain at lower levels. At the same time, several extreme outliers remind us that high migrant share alone does not automatically imply broad-based development.

3.1 Geographic Distribution of Migrant Share Outliers

This map examines the geographic distribution of outlier countries within the developing economies group in 2024, focusing on historical and geographic factors that may help explain their unusually high international migrant stock.

Using the 1.5×IQR rule, nine outlier countries were identified: Afghanistan, Angola, the United Arab Emirates, Argentina, Burundi, Benin, Burkina Faso, Bangladesh, and Bahrain. Rather than forming one single regional cluster, these countries are spread across Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and South America.

Their locations suggest that migration patterns are closely connected to each country’s historical and geographic context. Gulf countries such as the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain have long relied on foreign labor migration, while countries such as Afghanistan, Angola, and Burundi have been shaped by histories of conflict, displacement, and cross-border movement.

West African outliers such as Benin and Burkina Faso also reflect the importance of regional mobility and porous borders. Overall, the map shows that high migrant stock among developing economies is not only a statistical outlier pattern, but also a reflection of deeper historical experiences, geographic position, and regional migration systems.

4. Interactive Infant Mortality Visualizations

While the previous infant mortality map provides a geographic snapshot of global health outcomes, the following interactive visualizations expand the analysis by examining how infant mortality changes across countries, sex categories, and time.

These dashboards allow users to explore long-term health transition, compare regional development patterns, and observe how sex-based mortality inequality behaves as countries modernize.

Dashboard 1 — Infant Mortality and Health Transition Explorer

This interactive dashboard combines a global infant mortality map with a health transition scatter plot. Users can filter by country, region, year, and sex category to examine how infant mortality rates decline over time and how countries move through broader stages of health development.

The scatter plot specifically visualizes developmental transition by plotting total infant mortality against the male–female mortality gap. As countries develop, most shift toward lower mortality levels, although the pace of transition differs substantially across regions.

Dashboard 2 — Male–Female Infant Mortality Gap

This visualization isolates the male–female infant mortality gap across countries and over time. Positive values indicate higher male infant mortality, while negative values indicate higher female infant mortality.

By focusing specifically on the mortality difference itself, the chart highlights where sex-based inequality persists geographically even as overall infant mortality improves worldwide.

Overall Findings

Together, these visualizations demonstrate that infant mortality is not only a strong indicator of development, but also a way to examine global inequality and health transition over time.

Although most countries experienced substantial declines in infant mortality between 1960 and 2024, the pace of improvement differed significantly across regions and stages of development.

The dashboards also show that sex-based disparities persist even as countries modernize and improve healthcare systems, suggesting that development is a dynamic and uneven process rather than a uniform global transition.

Outro

This project demonstrates that development is multidimensional and cannot be fully understood through income-based classifications alone. By comparing fertility, infant mortality, migrant share, and other demographic indicators across countries and development groups, we observe that social outcomes often reveal important differences that traditional economic measures may overlook.

Together, the visualizations suggest that development should be viewed as a broader social process shaped by healthcare access, demographic transition, migration patterns, and long-term structural change.
