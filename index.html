New Developed Country Criteria

Spring Park1, Joonseop Jang1, and Oscar Molina Romualdo1
1George Mason University Korea

Abstract

Countries are often labeled as “developed” using income-based thresholds such as GNI per capita. However, income alone may not fully capture the broader social conditions associated with development. In this project, we examine whether social indicators such as fertility, infant mortality, migrant share, aging, and service-sector employment align with conventional GNI-based classifications. Using World Bank World Development Indicators and country groupings from the UN World Economic Situation and Prospects 2025 framework, we compare patterns across 175 countries. Our goal is to show that development is multidimensional and that a broader social development perspective may reveal important differences that income-based definitions overlook.

Project Motivation

The traditional definition of a developed country usually relies on national income. This approach is convenient and widely used, but it can be too narrow. A country may cross an income threshold while still facing serious demographic, health, or structural challenges. Likewise, some countries may display social characteristics commonly associated with development even if their income level alone does not fully reflect that progress.

Our project asks a simple question: should developed country status be defined by income alone, or should it also include social outcomes? To answer this, we visualize how several social indicators relate to established country classifications and then propose a broader Social Development Index as an alternative lens.

Data and Method

We use the most recent available data from the World Bank World Development Indicators (WDI) for 175 countries. For country group classifications, we follow the UN World Economic Situation and Prospects (WESP) 2025 framework, which separates countries into developed economies, economies in transition, and developing economies.

We focus on five social dimensions that are commonly linked to development:

  • Low fertility
  • Low infant mortality
  • Higher international migrant share
  • Population aging
  • Larger service-sector share

The visualizations below compare these indicators across country groups and help us evaluate whether development is better understood as a multidimensional social process rather than a purely income-based label.

1. Fertility and Development

Fertility decline is one of the most widely observed demographic patterns associated with development. Lower fertility often emerges alongside urbanization, women’s education, rising living costs, and expanded access to healthcare and family planning.

We expect developed economies to cluster at lower fertility levels, while many developing economies remain higher. However, the chart also reveals important variation: some middle-income countries have already undergone major fertility declines, while some wealthy oil-producing states may not fit a simple demographic pattern. This suggests that demographic transition is strongly related to development, but not perfectly captured by income category alone.

2. Migrant Share by Development Group

This violin plot compares international migrant stock as a share of total population across developed economies, economies in transition, and developing economies. Migration can reflect labor-market attraction, economic opportunity, political stability, and global integration, all of which are often associated with development.

Developed economies generally show the highest migrant shares and the greatest variation. Economies in transition tend to fall in the middle, while most developing economies remain at lower levels. At the same time, several extreme outliers, such as Gulf countries, remind us that high migrant share alone does not automatically imply broad-based development.

4. Interactive Infant Mortality Visualizations

While the previous infant mortality map provides a geographic snapshot of global health outcomes, the following interactive visualizations expand the analysis by examining how infant mortality changes across countries, sex categories, and time. These dashboards allow users to explore long-term health transition, compare regional development patterns, and observe how sex-based mortality inequality behaves as countries modernize.

Dashboard 1 & 2 — Infant Mortality and Health Transition Explorer

This interactive dashboard combines a global infant mortality map with a health transition scatter plot. Users can filter by country, region, year, and sex category to examine how infant mortality rates decline over time and how countries move through broader stages of health development.

The scatter plot specifically visualizes developmental transition by plotting total infant mortality against the male–female mortality gap. As countries develop, most shift toward lower mortality levels, although the pace of transition differs substantially across regions. Together, the visualizations demonstrate that health transition is dynamic, uneven, and closely connected to broader patterns of development and inequality.

Dashboard 3 — Male–Female Infant Mortality Gap

This visualization isolates the male–female infant mortality gap across countries and over time. Positive values indicate higher male infant mortality, while negative values indicate higher female infant mortality. By focusing specifically on the mortality difference itself, the chart highlights where sex-based inequality persists geographically even as overall infant mortality improves worldwide.

The divergence-style choropleth map allows viewers to examine how demographic inequality changes historically across regions and reinforces the idea that improvements in healthcare and development do not always eliminate inequality uniformly.

Overall Findings

Together, these visualizations demonstrate that infant mortality is not only a strong indicator of development, but also a way to examine global inequality and health transition over time. Although most countries experienced substantial declines in infant mortality between 1960 and 2024, the pace of improvement differed significantly across regions and stages of development.

The dashboards also show that sex-based disparities persist even as countries modernize and improve healthcare systems, suggesting that development is a dynamic and uneven process rather than a uniform global transition.

Outro

This project demonstrates that development is multidimensional and cannot be fully understood through income-based classifications alone. By comparing fertility, infant mortality, migrant share, and other demographic indicators across countries and development groups, we observe that social outcomes often reveal important differences that traditional economic measures may overlook.

Together, the visualizations suggest that development should be viewed as a broader social process shaped by healthcare access, demographic transition, migration patterns, and long-term structural change. Rather than relying solely on GNI per capita thresholds, a multidimensional approach may provide a more complete understanding of how countries experience and sustain development over time.
